oh well

this is a blog post documenting what i ended up presenting as my final project for physical computing. i am mainly doing this blog post to fulfill the requirements of the class, because, in it's current state, there really isn't anything worth showing to the world.

references

the goal was to realize a tool similar to the one presented here i.e. some kind of midi-controlled way to mechanically percuss any object/surface in the world at high enough frequencies to produce pitch, but also maintain the ability to create (slower) rhythmic patterns.

i will begin by saying that during the 2nd user-testing session, Tom Igoe informed me that these musical/mechanistic ideas are brillantly explored in the work of Nick Yulman particularly in the "Thing Synth" of the Bricolo music making system.

after feeling very unoriginal for two days, i took Igoe's advice and sent an email to Yulman to beg for advice and tips. i'm extremely grateful that he obliged by doing a phone call with me soon after. although i half turned it into an existential grad-school-counseling session, he gave me some very useful tips on things like overdriving solenoids and considerations about durablity of enclosures.

another reference i have been mentioning since the start of this project is an all-cardboard (or paper) acoustic synthesizer where the keys pushed card material to a cone of gears with different amount of teeth along one axis. i have not been able to find this on the internet anymore. if you are reading this and know what i'm talking about, i would be incredibly appreciative if you sent a link my way.

process

there were many mistakes on this road to failure

like i said before, i first began by exploring dc motoros/a rotational idea.

after foolishly trying to get by with an dc motor, i splurged for two motoros from servocity. with more torque

i could get a sound if i used a gear to pluck some kind of thing card, but it seemed to be too slow for any kind of audible pitch

i started thinking about how i could get something very quick, but still high-pressure. i thought there'd be some promise in some kind of rebounding mechanism. i began imagining some kind of environment where high pressure air is used to push some kind of bouncy but hard rubber ball to the end of a shaft (but not so much that the ball flies out). the ball would then bounce back a bit, until the air make it percuss again.

i then found out this is kind of how a jackhammer works, whicih scared me but then led me to try to epxlore solenoids more. if anything, the pervasiveness of using solenoids in an electro-acoustic musical practice seemed like a good reason to get more hands-on experience when them.

sadly i did not get a video of this, but because i could not get a transistor working for one of the user-testing sessions (because i mistakenly thought transistors were an electronic but still digital version of a realy, only outputing high voltage or no voltage and nothing in between) i used a relay. i presented it with a bunch of materials to play with, such as concrete, cardboard, acryllic, and paper (really just a pile of garbage in the corner in the room. loud garbage).

as the semester approached its end, i decided to focus my efforts on figuring out the transistors, because the speed of the pulse wave percussion was vital to coordinate mulitple motors at a time.

i eventually learned of the analog nature of transistors by hooking up a knob

i then tried to test the digital logic by putting in buttons to trigger the application of 12 volts to the solenoid. i'll skip ahead below by showing it with two solenoids instead of just one.

once i got that, i worked on getting code that just aims to fulfill pulsing at a target frequency

i made the code listen to serial, which i wrote to using node.js server with 

serialport
to send to the arduino and 
node-osc
to listen to ableton

in ableton i used the ableton connection kit to send simple osc messages to my node server

future plans

  • i'd like to explore vollying with more than two solenoids. i don't think i will want to do more than four though, because the pick-up-the-motor-directly nature is important to me. maybe i will be able to learn how to do clock signals so one could do 16 if they wanted, by using 4 of my 4-solenoid ones at once.

  • having become it a self-contained midi device (no node/websocket/osc server or ableton max4live plugin for osc-out needed) is essential to me

  • with slightly more experience (i.e. more experience than zero) with motors now, i'd like to continue exploring the possibility of the gear/rotational based idea.
